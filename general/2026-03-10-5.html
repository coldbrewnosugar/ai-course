Generate AI Videos with Open-Sora 2.0's Open-Source Pipeline

Set up and run an 11B open-source video generation model locally, from text prompts to rendered clips
40 min video-generation open-source diffusion-transformers text-to-video 3d-vae rectified-flow

What's happening

On March 12, 2025, the HPC-AI Tech team released Open-Sora 2.0 — an 11-billion parameter video generation model whose outputs match proprietary systems like HunyuanVideo (11B) and Step-Video (30B) on standardised benchmarks (VBench) and human preference studies. The entire stack — model checkpoints, training code, and inference pipeline — is open-source, and the team reports a total training cost of approximately $200,000.

This matters for several reasons. First, video generation has been one of the last frontiers where open-source alternatives lagged significantly behind proprietary offerings. Second, Open-Sora 2.0 provides a complete, reproducible pipeline: data preprocessing, training with acceleration, and inference with a Gradio demo. Third, the architecture — a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) backbone paired with a 3D Variational Autoencoder (3D-VAE) and rectified flow scheduling — represents the current state of the art for open video generation and is worth understanding on its own terms.

In this session, we will use an AI agent to help us install Open-Sora, understand its architecture, configure inference runs for both text-to-video and image-to-video generation, and engineer effective prompts for producing coherent multi-second clips.

1

Understanding the Architecture: DiT, 3D-VAE, and Rectified Flow

Before touching any installation commands, we need a working mental model of what Open-Sora 2.0 actually does when it generates a video. Three components form the core of the system, and understanding their roles will inform every decision we make later — from GPU selection to prompt construction.

The 3D Variational Autoencoder (3D-VAE) compresses raw video frames into a compact latent representation. Unlike a standard image VAE that operates on individual frames, the 3D-VAE processes both spatial dimensions (height and width) and the temporal dimension (time) simultaneously. This means it captures motion patterns directly in the latent space, rather than treating a video as a stack of independent images. The compression factor is substantial: a short video clip that would occupy gigabytes as raw pixel data is reduced to a latent tensor small enough for the diffusion process to operate on efficiently.

The Diffusion Transformer (DiT) is the generative backbone. Traditional diffusion models used U-Net architectures; Open-Sora 2.0 instead uses a transformer, which scales more predictably with parameter count and handles long-range dependencies (both spatial and temporal) more naturally. The 11B-parameter DiT receives the noisy latent representation, the text conditioning from a language encoder, and a timestep embedding, then predicts how to denoise the latent toward the target video.

Rectified flow is the scheduling strategy that governs the denoising process. Classical diffusion models learn to reverse a gradual noising process, step by step, from pure noise to clean data. Rectified flow straightens the paths between noise and data distributions, allowing the model to traverse from noise to a finished video in fewer sampling steps. Practically, this means faster inference without proportional quality loss.

Warning
Open-Sora 2.0's 11B model requires a GPU with at least 40 GB of VRAM (an A100-40GB or better) for standard inference. Lower-VRAM cards may work with model offloading or reduced resolution, but expect significantly longer generation times.
How does rectified flow actually 'straighten' the path?

In standard diffusion, the forward process adds Gaussian noise incrementally over many timesteps, creating curved trajectories in the data-noise space. The reverse process must learn to follow these curved paths, which requires many steps for accurate reconstruction. Rectified flow redefines the forward process as a linear interpolation between data and noise: at timestep t, the noisy sample is simply (1 - t) data + t noise. This creates straight-line trajectories that are inherently easier to learn and follow, meaning the model can take larger steps during sampling without accumulating error. The result is equivalent or better quality in fewer denoising iterations — a direct reduction in compute cost at inference time.

Why a Transformer instead of a U-Net?

U-Nets dominated early diffusion model architectures because their encoder-decoder structure with skip connections was well-suited to image-to-image tasks. However, transformers offer two key advantages for video generation: (1) self-attention naturally captures long-range dependencies across both spatial and temporal dimensions without requiring explicit architectural provisions, and (2) transformer performance scales more predictably with parameter count, following established scaling laws. The DiT architecture treats the latent tensor as a sequence of patches (analogous to Vision Transformer patches), applies full self-attention, and injects conditioning information through adaptive layer normalisation (adaLN). This unified approach handles spatial, temporal, and cross-modal (text) attention within a single architecture.

Installing Open-Sora and Downloading the 11B Checkpoint

With the architecture understood, we now turn to the practical matter of getting Open-Sora running locally. The installation process involves cloning the repository, setting up a Python environment with the correct dependencies, and downloading the model checkpoint from Hugging Face.

The dependency stack is nontrivial: Open-Sora requires PyTorch (with CUDA support), several HPC-AI Tech libraries (ColossalAI for distributed training support), the Hugging Face ecosystem (transformers, diffusers, accelerate), and various video processing utilities. Getting the versions right is important — mismatched CUDA or PyTorch versions are the most common source of installation failures.

What if I only have a 24 GB GPU?

The full 11B model at native precision requires approximately 40 GB of VRAM. On a 24 GB card (e.g., an RTX 4090), several strategies can bring the memory footprint within range:

  1. CPU offloading: Keep parts of the model in system RAM and swap them to GPU as needed. This trades speed for memory.
  2. Reduced precision: Running in float16 or bfloat16 (which Open-Sora supports natively) halves the memory footprint of the model weights.
  3. Reduced resolution and duration: Generating 480p clips instead of 720p, or 2-second clips instead of 5-second, reduces the latent tensor size significantly.
  4. The 1B model: Open-Sora 1.3 offers a 1B-parameter variant that produces lower-quality but still respectable results and fits comfortably on consumer GPUs.

Running Text-to-Video Inference

With the model installed, we can now generate our first video from a text prompt. Open-Sora's inference pipeline accepts a text prompt, a set of generation parameters (resolution, duration, number of sampling steps, guidance scale), and produces an output video file.

The key parameters that govern output quality and computational cost are:

  • Resolution: The spatial dimensions of the output video (e.g., 720×1280, 576×1024, 480×854). Higher resolutions require proportionally more VRAM and compute.
  • Number of frames / duration: More frames mean longer videos but also larger latent tensors.
  • Sampling steps: The number of denoising iterations. Rectified flow allows fewer steps than traditional schedulers — 30-50 steps typically suffice, compared to 100+ for DDPM.
  • Guidance scale (CFG): Controls how strongly the model follows the text prompt versus generating freely. Higher values produce outputs more faithful to the prompt but can reduce diversity and introduce artefacts.

The inference command is typically invoked through a Python script provided in the repository, with parameters passed either as command-line arguments or through a YAML configuration file.

Understanding classifier-free guidance in the video context

Classifier-free guidance (CFG) works by running the denoising step twice at each iteration: once with the text condition and once without (the 'unconditional' pass). The final prediction is a weighted combination: output = unconditional + scale * (conditional - unconditional). A higher scale amplifies the difference between conditioned and unconditioned outputs, pushing the generation more strongly toward the text description.

In video generation, this has a temporal dimension: CFG affects not just the appearance of individual frames but the coherence of motion. Excessively high guidance can cause the model to 'over-fit' to individual frame descriptions at the expense of smooth motion, producing videos where objects snap between poses rather than moving naturally. A scale of 7.0-9.0 is a practical starting range for Open-Sora 2.0.

Image-to-Video Generation and Conditioning

Open-Sora 2.0 supports not only text-to-video generation but also image-to-video (I2V) generation, where a static image serves as the first frame and the model generates subsequent frames that animate it. This capability is particularly useful when you need precise control over the starting composition — something that pure text prompts cannot always guarantee.

The I2V pipeline works by encoding the input image through the 3D-VAE encoder to obtain its latent representation, then using this as the initial condition for the denoising process. The text prompt still plays a role: it describes the desired motion and scene evolution, while the image anchors the visual content.

This dual-conditioning approach — image for appearance, text for dynamics — is a powerful pattern that extends to other modalities in the video generation literature (e.g., video-to-video style transfer, depth-conditioned generation). Understanding how Open-Sora handles it provides a foundation for working with more complex conditioning schemes.

Tip
When writing I2V prompts, describe what changes, not what exists. The image already encodes the scene's appearance. Your prompt should specify camera movement, object motion, lighting transitions, or environmental changes — the temporal dimension that the static image lacks.
Chaining image generation and video generation models

A practical production workflow chains two models:

  1. Image generation (FLUX, Stable Diffusion 3, or Midjourney): Generate a high-quality starting frame with precise compositional control. These models excel at spatial arrangement, style, and detail.
  2. Video generation (Open-Sora I2V): Animate the generated frame with natural motion.

This two-stage approach compensates for a fundamental tension in current video models: they are better at generating plausible motion than at precisely controlling the composition of any single frame. By decoupling composition (image model) from animation (video model), you get the best of both systems. The key constraint is resolution matching — generate the input image at the same aspect ratio and resolution you plan to use for video output.

Prompt Engineering for Video: Temporal Descriptions and VBench Evaluation

Prompting a video model differs fundamentally from prompting an image model. An image prompt describes a scene at a single moment; a video prompt must describe how a scene evolves over time. The most effective Open-Sora prompts include three categories of information:

  1. Scene description: The setting, objects, lighting, and visual style — similar to an image prompt.
  2. Temporal dynamics: What moves, how it moves, and over what duration. Camera motion (pan, tilt, zoom, tracking shot), object motion (a bird taking flight, waves crashing), and environmental changes (sunset progression, clouds moving).
  3. Cinematic language: Shot type (wide shot, close-up, aerial), camera movement style (steady, handheld), and visual mood (dramatic, serene, energetic). Video models trained on captioned footage respond well to cinematographic terminology.

Open-Sora's results on VBench — a standardised benchmark for video generation quality — provide insight into its strengths. VBench evaluates multiple dimensions: subject consistency, background consistency, temporal flickering, motion smoothness, aesthetic quality, and text-image alignment. Open-Sora 2.0's on-par performance with HunyuanVideo across these metrics indicates strong temporal coherence, but like all current models, it handles complex multi-object interactions less reliably than single-subject scenes.

What VBench actually measures

VBench decomposes video quality into 16 distinct dimensions grouped into two categories:

Video Quality: subject consistency (does the subject maintain its identity across frames?), background consistency, temporal flickering, motion smoothness, dynamic degree (how much motion occurs), aesthetic quality, and imaging quality.

Video-Text Alignment: overall consistency with the prompt, object class accuracy, multiple objects handling, spatial relationships, scene recognition, colour accuracy, and appearance style.

Open-Sora 2.0's parity with HunyuanVideo on VBench is significant because it demonstrates that open-source models can now match proprietary systems on rigorous, multi-dimensional evaluation. However, VBench scores are aggregates — a model may excel at motion smoothness while struggling with multiple-object scenes. Understanding which dimensions a model handles well allows you to write prompts that play to its strengths.

Recap

This session covered the complete pipeline for running Open-Sora 2.0, from architectural understanding through practical inference. We examined how the 3D-VAE, Diffusion Transformer, and rectified flow scheduler work together to produce temporally coherent video from text descriptions. We installed the system, ran both text-to-video and image-to-video generation, and developed a framework for writing effective video prompts.

The broader significance of Open-Sora 2.0 extends beyond its immediate capabilities. It demonstrates that the gap between open-source and proprietary video generation systems is closing rapidly — the $200,000 training cost, while nontrivial, is orders of magnitude less than what was required just two years ago. For practitioners, this means video generation is becoming an accessible tool rather than a gated service, and understanding how to operate these systems locally will be increasingly valuable.

  • The 3D-VAE's temporal compression is what makes video diffusion fundamentally different from running image diffusion frame-by-frame — it encodes motion in the latent space, enabling coherent temporal generation.
  • Rectified flow scheduling straightens the denoising trajectory, allowing 30-50 sampling steps to achieve quality that would require 100+ steps with traditional DDPM — a direct reduction in generation time.
  • Effective video prompts require three components: scene description, temporal dynamics (what moves and how), and cinematic language (shot type, camera movement). Prompts of 2-4 sentences tend to outperform both shorter and longer alternatives.
  • Image-to-video generation decouples composition from animation — a powerful workflow when combined with dedicated image generation models for precise first-frame control.
  • VBench provides a multi-dimensional evaluation framework for video quality; understanding which dimensions a model handles well allows you to write prompts that exploit its strengths.
