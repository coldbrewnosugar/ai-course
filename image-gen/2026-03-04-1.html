A $1,500 Text-to-Image Model — Trained in a Single Day

Photoroom just open-sourced the full recipe. Let's break it apart, understand every trick, and learn how to reproduce it with an AI agent at your side.
40 min diffusion-models text-to-image pixel-space-training perceptual-loss open-source

What's happening

Here's something that would've been unthinkable two years ago: Photoroom's PRX team just trained a text-to-image diffusion model from scratch in 24 hours on 32 H200 GPUs, for roughly $1,500 in total compute.

To put that in perspective — early Stable Diffusion training runs cost hundreds of thousands of dollars. DALL·E 2's training was estimated in the millions. We've gone from "only Big Tech can do this" to "a determined team with a credit card can do this over a weekend."

The secret isn't a single breakthrough. It's stacking a dozen clever tricks that each shave off time, improve convergence, or both. The biggest conceptual shift? Ditching the VAE entirely and training directly on pixels. That one decision unlocks a cascade of simplifications.

Photoroom open-sourced everything — the training code, the experimental framework, the full recipe. Today we're going to understand why each piece matters, and use an AI agent to help us explore, reproduce, and extend their work.

1

The Big Idea: Predict Pixels, Not Noise

Okay, let's start with the conceptual foundation — because honestly, this is the most interesting part.

Traditional diffusion models work in latent space. You take an image, compress it through a VAE (variational autoencoder) into a smaller representation, add noise to that, and train a model to remove the noise. Then at inference time, you denoise and decode back to pixels.

Think of it like trying to learn cooking by only ever tasting food through a straw. The VAE is the straw — it gives you some information, but you're always working through an intermediary.

X-prediction flips this. Instead of predicting the noise that was added, the model directly predicts what the clean image looks like. And because you're predicting actual pixels, you can skip the VAE entirely.

Why does this matter?

  • Simpler pipeline — no VAE to train, license, or debug
  • Clean images live on the data manifold — predicting something that "looks like a real image" is fundamentally easier than predicting random noise
  • You unlock classical CV tricks — perceptual losses, LPIPS, DINO features — all designed for pixel space

The key math is surprisingly clean: instead of predicting velocity v = x - ε, you predict x₀ directly and derive velocity from that. Let's get your agent to help you build intuition here.

But wait — doesn't predicting x₀ at high noise give you blurry images?

Great instinct! Yes — early in training and at high noise levels, the model's x₀ predictions will be blurry averages. But that's actually fine for two reasons:

  1. During training, the loss still provides useful gradients because perceptual losses care about structure, not pixel-perfect sharpness
  2. During inference, the iterative denoising process means early steps just need a rough direction — the model refines details in later steps when noise is low

This is one of those cases where what seems like a bug is actually a feature. The "blurry prediction" at high noise is the model correctly expressing uncertainty about details while nailing the global structure.

Patching: How to Feed a 1024px Image to a Transformer

Here's a practical problem: a 1024×1024 image has over a million pixels. Transformers run on tokens, and attention is quadratic in sequence length. You can't just feed a million tokens into a transformer — your GPU would catch fire.

The solution is patching — chop the image into square patches and treat each patch as a single token. It's the same idea as Vision Transformers (ViT), just applied to diffusion.

Photoroom uses 32×32 pixel patches, which is much larger than the typical 16×16 or even 8×8 you see in classification models. Here's the napkin math:

  • At 512px: (512 / 32)² = 256 tokens — very manageable!
  • At 1024px: (1024 / 32)² = 1024 tokens — still totally fine

Compare that to 8×8 patches at 1024px: (1024 / 8)² = 16,384 tokens. That's the difference between a 2-minute training step and a 2-hour one.

The tradeoff? Larger patches mean less spatial detail per token. To compensate, they use a 256-dimensional bottleneck in the projection layer — the patch gets compressed to 256 dims before entering the transformer, keeping the model size reasonable while the transformer learns global relationships.

Think of it like Google Maps zoom levels. You don't need street-level detail to plan a cross-country road trip. The 32px patches give you the "highway view" — the transformer figures out the big picture, and the details emerge during iterative denoising.

What the agent gives back

Tip
The 256-dim bottleneck is doing important work here. Without it, projecting a 32×32×3 = 3,072-dimensional patch directly into the transformer's hidden dimension would waste capacity on raw pixel values. The bottleneck forces the model to learn a compact representation right at the input layer.
Why not use even larger patches like 64×64?

At 64×64 patches on a 512px image, you'd get (512/64)² = 64 tokens. That's barely any spatial information — the transformer would essentially see an 8×8 grid of super-blurry tiles. There's a sweet spot where you have enough tokens for the transformer to reason about spatial relationships but few enough to keep training fast. For this architecture and resolution range, 32px hits that sweet spot.

Also worth noting: the patch size interacts with the multi-resolution training schedule. At 512px with 32px patches, 256 tokens is comfortable. When they jump to 1024px, the 1024 tokens is still within what modern transformers handle well. If they'd used 16px patches, the 1024px stage would need 4,096 tokens — still doable but significantly more expensive.

✓
At this point you should understand two core ideas: (1) x-prediction lets you train directly on pixels by predicting the clean image instead of noise, and (2) large 32px patches keep the token count manageable — 256 tokens at 512px, 1024 at 1024px. These two decisions together are what make a 24-hour speedrun even thinkable.

Perceptual Losses: Borrowing Eyes from Classical CV

Here's where pixel-space training really pays off. When your model predicts actual pixels, you can use perceptual losses — and these have been refined over a decade of computer vision research.

A quick refresher: the standard diffusion loss (MSE between predicted and target) treats every pixel equally. A one-pixel shift of an entire image would score terribly, even though the image looks identical to humans. Perceptual losses fix this by comparing images through the "eyes" of a pretrained neural network.

Photoroom uses two perceptual losses stacked on top of the diffusion objective:

  1. LPIPS (weight: 0.1) — This uses a pretrained VGG network to compare low-level features: edges, textures, local patterns. Think of it as checking "does the surface look right?"
  1. DINO perceptual loss (weight: 0.01) — This uses DINOv2, a self-supervised vision model, to compare high-level semantic features. Think of it as checking "is this the right thing?"

Notice the weight difference — LPIPS gets 10× more weight than DINO. That's intentional. You want the model to primarily learn the diffusion objective, with perceptual losses as gentle nudges. Too much perceptual weight and the model starts hallucinating features the perceptual networks are biased toward.

The beautiful part: these losses add almost no compute overhead. The VGG and DINO forward passes are tiny compared to the main transformer forward pass. Free lunch, basically.

Ask your agent to explain why perceptual losses are awkward in latent-space diffusion but natural in pixel-space, using a concrete example.

  • What does the model output look like in latent space vs. pixel space?
  • If your model outputs a latent vector, what extra step would you need before computing LPIPS?

What the agent gives back

The agent should explain that in latent-space models, the output is an abstract vector — to compute perceptual loss, you'd have to decode it back to pixels through the VAE first. That means backpropagating through the VAE decoder, which is expensive and can cause gradient issues (the VAE wasn't trained for this). In pixel space, the model's output IS an image — you just feed it directly into VGG or DINO. No adapter, no extra decode step, no gradient hacks. The agent might use an analogy like: 'It's the difference between judging a painting directly vs. judging a description of a painting that someone else has to interpret first.'

Warning
The DINO loss is applied on pooled full images, not patch-wise. This is important — patch-wise DINO features might push the model to optimize local patches independently, breaking global coherence. Pooling ensures the semantic loss captures the whole-image meaning.
How do these losses interact with different noise levels?

This is a subtle but important detail. Both perceptual losses are applied at all noise levels — even when the image is mostly noise and the model's x₀ prediction is a blurry mess.

At high noise, the LPIPS loss can't really help (the prediction is too blurry for texture comparison to be meaningful). But the DINO loss still provides a useful signal — even a blurry blob can have the right semantic content ('this is roughly a dog shape').

At low noise, both losses shine. LPIPS ensures textures and edges are sharp and realistic, while DINO ensures the semantic content hasn't drifted.

The authors of PixelGen (which inspired this approach) showed that this combination noticeably improves both convergence speed and final visual quality. More signal at every training step = faster learning.

The Speed Stack: Token Routing, REPA, and Muon

Alright, so we've got the conceptual foundation: predict pixels, use big patches, add perceptual losses. But we need to go faster. The 24-hour budget is tight. This is where the engineering gets fun.

Photoroom stacks three more acceleration tricks:

TREAD (Token Routing) — Not every token needs to go through every transformer block. TREAD routes 50% of tokens from the 2nd block directly to the penultimate block, skipping all the middle layers. Think of it like an express lane on a highway — some cars (tokens) take the scenic route through every block, others take the express bypass. This cuts compute roughly in half for those tokens while barely affecting quality.

REPA (Representation Alignment) — At the 8th transformer block, they align the model's internal representations with a pretrained DINOv3 teacher. It's like having a tutor who whispers "you're on the right track" at a specific checkpoint during the forward pass. This helps the model learn good internal features faster. Weight: 0.5, which is actually quite high — they're taking this signal seriously.

Muon Optimizer — Instead of plain AdamW, they use Muon for all 2D parameters (weight matrices) and Adam only for 1D parameters (biases, norms). Muon uses Nesterov momentum with a special Newton-Schulz orthogonalization step. The practical upshot: better conditioning of the optimization landscape, which means faster convergence.

The training schedule is two stages:

  • Stage 1: 100k steps at 512px, batch size 1024
  • Stage 2: 20k steps at 1024px, batch size 512

Start at lower resolution where training is cheap, lock in the core capabilities, then fine-tune at high resolution.

API Key Note
If you want to actually run this training, you'll need access to 32 H200 GPUs. Most cloud providers offer these — Lambda Labs, RunPod, and CoreWeave are popular options. At ~$2/GPU/hour, budget around $1,500 for the full 24-hour run.
What's self-guidance and why does it replace CFG?

Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) is the standard trick for improving prompt adherence in diffusion models. It works by running the model twice — once with the text condition, once without — and amplifying the difference. The problem: it literally doubles your inference compute.

With TREAD's token routing, they get a natural alternative called self-guidance. The "dense" forward pass (all tokens through all blocks) gives the conditional prediction. The "routed" forward pass (50% of tokens taking the express lane) gives a weaker, partially-unconditional prediction. The difference between these two serves the same role as CFG — but you only need to run the model once, using the routing mechanism you already have.

It's not quite as powerful as full CFG, but it's free — no extra forward pass required. When you're counting every GPU-hour, that matters a lot.

Why synthetic training data?

The ~8.7M training images come from three synthetic datasets generated by Flux and Midjourney. This is increasingly common in 2025-2026 — synthetic data offers:

  1. Clean licensing — no copyright concerns from scraping the internet
  2. High quality captions — generated images come with their prompts, and can be re-captioned with models like Gemini 2.5 Flash
  3. Consistent quality — no blurry phone photos or watermarked stock images
  4. Diversity control — you can generate exactly the distribution you want

The flip side: your model can only be as diverse as the models that generated the training data. Photoroom acknowledges this — some limitations in their outputs (especially unusual subjects or compositions) come from data diversity rather than architectural issues.

✓
You now understand the full stack: x-prediction eliminates the VAE, 32px patches keep sequence length manageable, perceptual losses (LPIPS + DINO) provide multi-level supervision, TREAD routes 50% of tokens for speed, REPA aligns representations with a teacher model, and Muon optimizer improves convergence. These all combine to make a 24-hour training run produce usable text-to-image generation.

Reproduce It: Setting Up the PRX Training Framework

Let's get practical. Photoroom open-sourced everything on GitHub, so we can actually set this up and run it. Even if you don't have 32 H200s lying around, understanding the codebase structure teaches you a lot about how modern diffusion training pipelines work.

The repo contains two main things:

  1. The speedrun training code — the exact config that produced their 24-hour model
  2. The experimental framework from Parts 1 and 2 — so you can test individual tricks in isolation

What the agent gives back

The agent should estimate this would take 4-6 hours on 8×H100 and produce lower-quality but still coherent outputs — enough to validate the pipeline works before committing to a full run.

Tip
A great first experiment: train at 256px only with 16px patches (still 256 tokens). This lets you iterate fast on the loss functions and optimizer settings. Once those look right, scale up resolution and patch size together.
How do I evaluate whether my run is working?

During training, watch for these signals:

  1. Loss curves — The diffusion MSE should drop steadily. LPIPS and DINO losses should decrease but may be noisy.
  2. Sample quality — Generate samples every few thousand steps with fixed prompts. You should see: (a) random noise → blurry blobs by 5k steps, (b) recognizable objects by 20k steps, (c) coherent scenes by 50k steps.
  3. FID/CMMD scores — If you have a reference dataset, compute these periodically. Photoroom's 1024px pixel-space model hit FID 14.1, which is very competitive.
  4. Prompt adherence — Try progressively harder prompts. Simple ones ('a red car') should work early, complex ones ('a cat wearing a top hat riding a bicycle through Paris at sunset') take longer.

Known issues even in their full run: texture glitches, occasional anatomy problems, and struggles with very compositionally complex prompts. These are undertraining artifacts, not architectural problems — they'd likely improve with more compute and data.

Recap

Let's zoom out. We just walked through how a small team trained a text-to-image diffusion model from scratch in 24 hours for $1,500. Not by finding one magic trick, but by carefully stacking a dozen optimizations that each contribute a piece:

🧠 X-prediction — predict the clean image, not the noise. This puts predictions on the natural image manifold, making the learning problem fundamentally easier.

🖼️ Pixel-space training — ditch the VAE entirely. Simpler pipeline, no external dependencies, and it unlocks classical CV tools.

🧩 32px patches — keep the token sequence short (256 at 512px, 1024 at 1024px) so transformers stay fast.

👁️ Perceptual losses — LPIPS for texture quality, DINO for semantic correctness. Gentle weights so they guide without dominating.

🚀 TREAD routing — skip 50% of tokens through half the transformer blocks. Nearly 2× speedup with minimal quality loss.

📚 REPA alignment — use a DINOv3 teacher to accelerate feature learning at intermediate layers.

⚡ Muon optimizer — better optimization geometry for weight matrices means faster convergence.

📈 Resolution scheduling — 100k steps cheap at 512px, then 20k steps at 1024px to add detail.

The most important lesson isn't any single trick — it's the methodology. Each of these was tested in isolation (Parts 1 and 2 of the blog series), measured carefully, and only the winners made it into the final recipe. That's how you do applied ML research.

  • Predicting clean images (x-prediction) instead of noise makes pixel-space training viable, eliminating the VAE and simplifying the entire pipeline
  • Patch size is a critical hyperparameter — 32px patches at 1024px resolution give 1024 tokens, which is the sweet spot between detail and computational cost
  • Perceptual losses (LPIPS + DINO) are free in pixel space and dramatically improve convergence speed — they were awkward-to-impossible in latent space
  • Token routing (TREAD) can skip 50% of tokens through most transformer blocks with minimal quality loss — a near-2× speedup
  • Multi-resolution training (512px → 1024px) lets you get 80% of the learning done cheaply, then refine at high resolution
  • Modern optimizers like Muon, representation alignment, and self-guidance each contribute incremental gains that compound into dramatic speedups when stacked
